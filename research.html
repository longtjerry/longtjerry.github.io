Molecular Evolution of Primate Papillomaviruses

Hundreds of papillomavirus (PVs) genotype were characterized so far, but as we know, merely 13 human types were officially determined as high-risk types, which may cause cervical cancer. With curiosity to understand the genetic origin of such high-risk HPVs, my PhD study illustrated genomic diversity of non-human primate PVs and demonstrated that non-human primate PVs were sharing similar evolutionary history with HPVs. And Using E6-mediated p53 degradation as a model, I clarified the evolutionary relatedness of the carcinogenicity of human and non-human primate PVs, which provided the genetic proof of carcinogenic origin and further suggested the potentials of cross-species carcinogenic abilities of PVs

Novel papillomaviruses discovery

Long T,et al
We characterized the genome of the six novel Macaca mulatta PVs isolated from genital sites of rhesus monkeys that distributed in Hong Kong. Six novel genomes were named MmPV2 to MmPV7. The complete genome sequences of MmPV2 to MmPV7 were submitted to NCBI/GenBank under the accession numbers MG837557 to MG837559 and MH745747 to MH745749. The phylogenetic tree was deposited in figshare.


Genomic evolution relationship of primate PVs

Chen Z,et al
Our study supported the fact that the multiple PV ancestors had split and adapted to specific host niches within an ancestral host animal lineage, prior to the host speciation events. Following the subsequent divergence and niche adaptation, distinct but phylogenetically related PV types were transmitted to similar host ecosystems by closely related host animals when the host speciation occurred.


Evolutionary carcinogenicity of primate PVs

Long T,et al
We used E6-mediated p53 degradation as an in vitro model to support our observation that the most recent common ancestor of high-risk PV types has likely acquired phenotypic traits of carcinogenicity before speciation of host animals. However, the loss-of-function mechanism might be able to explain the formation of a host species barrier in carcinogenicty or even defective phenotypic traits.

Rabies Virus and Blood-brain Barrier


Rabies virus (RABV) infects hosts, most mammals, from the peripheral bite. Following the commencement of primary amplification in bite site, the infecting viruses adapted to peripheral nerve, and march to central nervous system (CNS). The hosts will be saved when the neutralizing antibodies manage to reach the brain tissue, from peripheral blood through blood-brain barrier (BBB). To understand the fatality of street RABV strain, we evaluated the genetic function of RABV phosphoprotein on BBB. Our study suggested that the phosphoprotein of lethal RABV strain plays as a role in lowering the enhancement of BBB permeability. Long T,et al
